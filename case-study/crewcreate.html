The problem

TechTurnUp, a nonprofit based in the DMV that runs after school, summer, and online STEAM programming for students, shared a few goals in the problem brief.

  • They wanted an online platform that was an extension of their in person programming and encouraged students to continue learning outside of class but did not overlap with existing online programming.
  • They shared that online safety had been an issue in the past (For example, a student once drew lewd images on the shared presentation in a very important meeting) and emphasised that safety would be integral to their platform.
  • Tim, the founder of TechTurnUp, shared in our interview that he had ambitions for the platform to be an "entrypoint" for people to join the TechTurnUp community as they expanded operations all over the world.
    For these goals, existing platforms like Scratch are too rigid, don't support original creation, and lack the safety infrastructure parents and facilitators actually need. We had 72 hours to go from brief to working prototype. We needed something engaging enough to retain attention, safe enough for parents to trust, and scaffolded well enough for kids with no prior exposure.

    My role

    I led design and strategy for the team. I proposed the space adventure rebrand that unified the visual identity and the gaming-lobby-style crew chat with moving characters. I designed the Missions page end-to-end and presented the information architecture, accessibility considerations, challenges and trade-offs, MVP scoping, and the future roadmap — including how the product scales technically and geographically. I also discovered TechTurnUp independently and brought them in as the Hack4Impact client for this makeathon.

    How I worked through it

    72 hours forces extreme prioritization - of 26 participating teams, only 13 made the submission deadline and presented. My team and I conducted the research and design phases in parallel.

    1

    User interviews & personas

    My team and I conducted interviews with TTU staff and students, synthesizing findings into three personas: Jacob (student, 15, wants to build his own game), Patricia (facilitator, stretched thin managing large cohorts), and Gabriela (parent, worried about safety and lack of visibility).

    3

    Information architecture, solution pillars & product vision

    My team and I researched existing solutions and found shortcomings worth addressing. We then mapped the full IA across six primary sections with sub-flows for each user type. We aligned on the three solution pillars: safety (AI content filtering, MFA with personalized security questions, facilitator supervision), engagement (Crews, Rewards, Gallery, participation nudges), and scalability (feature, geographic, and field-based expansion with a freemium model). We introduced the AI chatbot (Support Center) that keeps students from leaving the platform for YouTube or Reddit, removing a key safety risk. We fleshed out features and gathered visual assets to support the designing process. My team and I - with experiences spanning education, design and engineering - aligned on the goal of the platform to be a space for students to lead creative projects and develop product management skills early.

    2

    Prototyping and Space theme

    We started with the lo-fi prototyping, following which I proposed the space adventure theme — Launchpads, Missions, Crews, Flights — as the conceptual frame. The theme aligned with TTU's brand colors (purple and green) and gave the product a distinct identity in the hi-fi prototypes.

    5

    Missions page design

    I owned the Missions page end-to-end: Kanban board for task tracking, project setup with individual milestones, progress reports, role assignments, mentor feedback, and the Support Center. I also designed the Crew chat to feel like a gaming lobby with moving character avatars and a Campfire interface to make collaboration feel native to the experience.

    6

    Presentation - MVP scoping, accessibility & roadmap

    I presented the DVF analysis — desirability anchored in game design principles and student agency, viability through vertical integration with TTU's merch store Innovators' Threads, and feasibility by scoping out complex backend dependencies. I covered WCAG AA accessibility standards, challenges around age-range scaling and depth vs. breadth, and the future roadmap: starting in DMV, expanding to new regions and fields like Robotics and Music. I also covered the IA and Design process.

    What I learned

    A 72-hour sprint is a forcing function for everything product and design: talk to users first, decide fast, and commit fully to one direction.

    What worked

    Proposing the space theme after the lo-fi prototypes locked in a coherent design language before any screens were finalized. Every subsequent decision (colors, copy, IA naming) had a clear reference point.

    What I'd change

    The Missions page has a complex flow and I discovered some flows late into the design process. Our team worked asynchronously and independently for our individual page designs, but in the future I'd work collaboratively and prototype the hardest flow first so there's time to iterate and receive feedback real-time.

    Unexpected challenge

    Our presentation was pretty long, and we ended up not being able to present the last 3 slides in time despite being able to make it while rehearsing. In the future, I will optimize for tighter pitches with fewer words.

    Skill gained

    Completing the entire design process in 72 hrs; Pitching and answering questions about technical future roadmaps impromptu but in a grounded and honest manner.
