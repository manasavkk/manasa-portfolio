The problem

Kids in underserved communities have drastically less access to quality computer science education than their peers — not because the interest isn't there, but because the tools that exist weren't built for them. Existing coding platforms are either too complex for beginners, gamified in ways that don't resonate with older kids, or lack safety features that matter to parents and community organizations.

The hackathon brief came from a real community client: an organization working with youth in underserved DMV-area neighborhoods who needed a solution that was engaging enough to retain attention, safe enough for parents to trust, and scaffolded well enough that kids without prior exposure could actually make progress. We had 72 hours to go from brief to working prototype.

My role

I led design and research for the team. I owned the stakeholder interview process, facilitated the DVF analysis session that shaped our solution direction, built the design system in Figma, and designed the core prototype flows. On a cross-functional hackathon team, roles blur — I also contributed to the pitch narrative and helped structure the product's safety and moderation framework.

The team's engineers built a functional code from the prototype spec I produced; the judges explicitly cited the design quality and research grounding as differentiating factors in the first-place decision.

How I worked through it

72 hours forces extreme prioritization. I ran the research and design phases in parallel with the team's engineering work — no sequential handoffs, just constant alignment.

1

Stakeholder interviews — hours 0–8

I conducted 20+ interviews across three groups: community organization staff who would deploy the platform, parents of kids in the target age range, and kids themselves (via proxy questions to parents and org staff). The key insight from the first hour: parents' #1 concern wasn't engagement — it was safety and moderation. That reframed the entire product.

2

DVF analysis & solution direction — hours 8–12

I facilitated a structured DVF session with the team, mapping each solution concept against desirability (do users want it?), viability (can the client sustain it?), and feasibility (can we build it in 72 hours with a plausible path to production?). CrewCreate emerged as the strongest candidate — a gamified, crew-based coding environment where progress was social and moderated.

3

Design system & information architecture — hours 12–20

I built a lightweight design system in Figma: type scale, color tokens, component set (buttons, cards, badges, crew widgets). Working from a system rather than designing screens ad hoc meant I could produce prototype-quality screens much faster in the later sprint and keep visual consistency across every frame.

4

Core prototype flows — hours 20–56

I designed five key flows: onboarding, the crew formation and joining experience, a coding challenge module with scaffolded hints, the progress and badge system, and the moderation dashboard for org admins. Each flow was prototyped with realistic interactions in Figma so judges and stakeholders could experience it, not just look at static screens.

5

Pitch preparation & demo — hours 56–72

I structured the pitch narrative around the research — leading with a specific quote from a parent interview, walking through the DVF rationale, then demonstrating the prototype. The judges noted that the research grounding made the solution feel credible rather than speculative. We won first place and the client requested the prototype for implementation review.

What it achieved

In 72 hours the team went from zero to a research-grounded, fully interactive prototype — and won the competition. The work didn't end with the hackathon.

  • 🥇 1st
    First place at TechTurnUp × UXTerps × Hack4Impact hackathon, judged on research depth, solution quality, and prototype fidelity.
  • 20+
    Stakeholder interviews conducted in the first 8 hours — org staff, parents, and youth proxies — forming the research foundation for the solution.
  • CrewCreate prototype currently under client review for implementation — the client organization is evaluating it as a real product to deploy in their programs.

What I learned

A 72-hour sprint is a forcing function for everything product and design work should always do but rarely does: talk to users first, decide fast, and commit fully to one direction.

What worked

Building the design system before designing screens. It felt like it was "wasting" the first 8 hours of design time, but it made everything faster and more consistent from hour 20 onward. Under time pressure, a system pays back faster than it costs.

What I'd change

I would have defined the moderation flow earlier — it was the most technically complex part of the product and we scoped it late. The judges noticed it was less developed than the other flows. In a real sprint I'd identify the hardest flow on day one and prototype it first.

Unexpected challenge

Keeping the team aligned on direction during the middle hours. Everyone had opinions about features after the DVF session, and scope creep started setting in around hour 30. A clear "this is our MVP, everything else is future state" decision — made explicitly — would have reduced the friction.

Skill gained

Facilitating a DVF analysis under time pressure with a cross-functional team — getting engineers, designers, and a researcher to reach a shared prioritization decision in under two hours. That facilitation skill transfers directly to product planning in any context.
